## Why Emotional Intelligence Predicts Negotiation Success More Than IQ

In a landmark study, researchers at Stanford University analysed 100 MBA students in a negotiation exercise. The students with the highest IQs did not achieve the best outcomes. The students with the highest emotional intelligence (EQ) did — by a margin of 30-50%.

This finding has been replicated across industries, cultures, and negotiation types. EQ — the ability to recognise, understand, and manage your own emotions and those of others — is the single best predictor of negotiation success.

The reason is simple: negotiation is fundamentally a social interaction. It involves reading the other party, managing your own reactions, building trust, and influencing behaviour. These are emotional intelligence skills, not analytical skills.

This article provides a comprehensive framework for applying emotional intelligence in negotiation, drawing from Daniel Goleman's EQ model, Chris Voss's tactical empathy, neuroscience research on emotion and decision-making, and decades of negotiation research.

## 1. The Four Pillars of EQ in Negotiation

Daniel Goleman's model of emotional intelligence has four pillars, arranged from internal to external:

```
INTERNAL                          EXTERNAL
Self-Awareness  →  Self-Management  →  Social Awareness  →  Relationship Management
(Know yourself)   (Control yourself)  (Read others)        (Influence others)
```

### Pillar 1: Self-Awareness

**What it is:** The ability to recognise your own emotions, triggers, and patterns in real-time.

**In negotiation:** Self-awareness means knowing when you're becoming angry, anxious, or defensive — before those emotions drive your behaviour. It means recognising your biases (overconfidence, anchoring, loss aversion) and compensating for them.

**Example:** You're negotiating a contract and the other party makes a dismissive comment about your proposal. You feel a flash of anger. Without self-awareness, you snap back — damaging the relationship and the negotiation. With self-awareness, you notice the anger, take a breath, and respond calmly: "I hear your concern. Let me explain the reasoning behind that proposal."

**Developing self-awareness:**
- Keep an emotion journal: What triggered strong emotions during negotiations? How did you respond?
- Identify your top 3 negotiation triggers (e.g., being interrupted, being called "unreasonable," dismissive body language)
- Practice the "body scan" technique: Notice physical sensations (tight chest, clenched jaw, shallow breathing) that signal emotional activation
- Ask trusted colleagues: "What do you notice about my behaviour when I'm under pressure?"

### Pillar 2: Self-Management

**What it is:** The ability to control your emotional responses and behave intentionally rather than reactively.

**In negotiation:** Self-management means staying calm when provoked, patient when frustrated, and focused when distracted. It means not making decisions while emotionally activated.

**Example:** The other party uses an aggressive tactic — a personal attack, a "take it or leave it" ultimatum, or a deliberate provocation. Without self-management, you react emotionally — either capitulating (flight) or attacking back (fight). With self-management, you pause, breathe, and choose your response: "I understand you feel strongly about this. Let's take a 10-minute break and come back to it."

**Techniques for self-management:**
- **The 6-second rule:** When triggered, count to 6 before responding. This allows the prefrontal cortex to re-engage and override the amygdala response.
- **Tactical breathing:** Inhale for 4 seconds, hold for 4, exhale for 4, hold for 4. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces stress.
- **The "name it to tame it" technique:** Silently name your emotion: "I'm feeling angry." Naming the emotion activates the prefrontal cortex and reduces amygdala activation.
- **Pre-commitment:** Before the negotiation, write down: "If I feel [emotion], I will [action]." This creates a pre-committed response that doesn't require in-the-moment decision-making.

### Pillar 3: Social Awareness

**What it is:** The ability to read others' emotions, perspectives, and concerns — even when they're not explicitly stated.

**In negotiation:** Social awareness means reading the other party's emotional state through body language, tone, word choice, and what they don't say. It means understanding their interests, constraints, and pressures — even when they haven't shared them.

**Example:** During a negotiation, the other party's lead negotiator says "That's an interesting proposal" while leaning back, crossing arms, and avoiding eye contact. Without social awareness, you hear "interesting" and proceed. With social awareness, you read the closed body language and recognise resistance. You probe: "I sense you have some concerns. Can you share what you're thinking?"

**Developing social awareness:**
- **Active listening:** Focus on the speaker completely. Don't prepare your response while they're talking. Listen for emotion, not just content.
- **Body language reading:** Watch for micro-expressions, posture shifts, eye contact patterns, and hand movements. These reveal emotions that words don't.
- **Tone analysis:** Listen for changes in pitch, pace, and volume. A sudden pitch increase may signal surprise or discomfort. A pace decrease may signal careful thinking.
- **The "what's not said" technique:** Pay attention to what the other party avoids. If they dodge questions about timeline, timeline may be their pressure point. If they avoid discussing budget, budget may be their constraint.

### Pillar 4: Relationship Management

**What it is:** The ability to use emotional awareness to build trust, influence behaviour, and manage interactions productively.

**In negotiation:** Relationship management means using your emotional awareness to build rapport, defuse tension, influence the other party's behaviour, and guide the negotiation toward productive outcomes.

**Example:** You notice the other party is frustrated with the pace of negotiation. Instead of pushing harder, you acknowledge their frustration: "It seems like you're feeling the pace is too slow. I want to make sure we're using our time efficiently. What would help us move faster?" This acknowledgment defuses the emotion and invites collaboration.

**Techniques for relationship management:**
- **Tactical empathy (Voss):** Demonstrate understanding of the other party's feelings and perspective without necessarily agreeing. "It seems like you're under significant pressure from your board."
- **Labeling (Voss):** Name the other party's emotion to defuse it. "It sounds like budget certainty is critical for you." Labels are observations, not accusations.
- **Mirroring (Voss):** Repeat the last 1-3 words of the other party's statement as a question. This encourages elaboration and reveals information.
- **Calibrated questions:** Ask "how" and "what" questions that shift cognitive load to the other party. "How am I supposed to deliver that with the current scope?"
- **Strategic silence:** After making a key point or asking a question, stay silent. Let the weight of the statement land. Don't fill the silence.

## 2. The Neuroscience of Emotion in Negotiation

### The Amygdala Hijack

The amygdala is the brain's threat detection system. It processes emotional signals before the prefrontal cortex (the rational thinking area) can intervene. When the amygdala perceives a threat — including social threats like disrespect, loss of control, or unfairness — it triggers a fight, flight, or freeze response.

**In negotiation:** Aggressive tactics, personal attacks, or even seemingly innocuous comments can trigger an amygdala hijack. When this happens:
- The prefrontal cortex goes offline
- Rational thinking is impaired
- Tunnel vision narrows options
- Emotional reactions are disproportionate
- Decision quality degrades

**The implication:** The most important EQ skill in negotiation is preventing amygdala hijacks — yours and the other party's.

### Preventing Your Own Amygdala Hijack

1. **Preparation:** Thorough preparation reduces uncertainty, which reduces threat perception.
2. **Trigger awareness:** Know what triggers you. If being called "unreasonable" triggers you, prepare a response in advance.
3. **Physical regulation:** Deep breathing, grounded posture, and regular breaks keep the nervous system regulated.
4. **Cognitive reframing:** "This isn't an attack — it's a negotiation tactic." "This isn't personal — it's business."
5. **The pause:** When you feel the hijack coming, pause. Don't respond immediately. Count to 6. Breathe. Then respond.

### Preventing Their Amygdala Hijack

1. **Avoid threat triggers:** Don't attack, don't dismiss, don't condescend.
2. **Use tactical empathy:** "It seems like this is a sensitive issue." Acknowledgment reduces threat perception.
3. **Give autonomy:** "You decide." "What works for you?" Autonomy reduces the feeling of being controlled.
4. **Maintain respect:** Even in disagreement, maintain respectful tone and body language.
5. **Allow face-saving:** Never back the other party into a corner where admitting they're wrong would cause public embarrassment.

## 3. Reading the Other Party

### The Body Language Dictionary

**Open signals (engagement, receptivity):**
- Leaning forward
- Open arms (uncrossed)
- Maintaining eye contact
- Nodding
- Smiling (genuine — involves the eyes)
- Mirroring your posture

**Closed signals (resistance, discomfort):**
- Leaning back
- Crossed arms or legs
- Avoiding eye contact
- Fidgeting
- Fake smiling (mouth only, no eye involvement)
- Posture shifts away from you

**Stress signals (anxiety, pressure):**
- Touching the neck
- Lip pressing
- Jaw clenching
- Shallow breathing
- Pacing or restlessness
- Voice pitch increase

**Deception signals (caution — not definitive):**
- Sudden posture change
- Inconsistency between words and body language
- Excessive eye contact (overcompensation)
- Self-soothing gestures (touching face, rubbing hands)
- Verbal hedging ("to be honest," "frankly," "to tell you the truth")

**Important caveat:** Body language signals are not definitive. They are indicators that warrant further exploration. Never accuse based on body language alone — use it as a signal to ask more questions.

### Reading What's Not Said

Some of the most important information in a negotiation is what the other party doesn't say:

- **Topics they avoid:** If they dodge budget questions, budget is a constraint. If they avoid timeline questions, timeline is a pressure point.
- **Questions they don't ask:** If they don't ask about implementation, they may not be serious. If they don't ask about risk, they may not understand the project.
- **People they don't mention:** If they never mention their boss, the boss may not be supportive. If they never mention other stakeholders, there may be hidden decision-makers.
- **Concessions they don't request:** If they don't ask for price reduction, price may not be their primary concern. If they don't ask for delivery guarantees, they may have alternative supply.

## 4. Real Business Examples

### Construction

A project manager is negotiating a variation order with the employer's representative. The rep is dismissive: "This should have been included in the original scope."

**Without EQ:** The PM argues: "It wasn't in the scope. The ground conditions were unforeseeable. We're entitled to the variation." The rep becomes defensive. The negotiation becomes adversarial.

**With EQ:** The PM reads the rep's frustration (body language: crossed arms, leaning back, tight voice). The PM uses labeling: "It seems like you're concerned about budget overruns." The rep opens up: "Yes, my boss is all over me about the budget. Every variation goes to him for approval." Now the PM understands the constraint: the rep isn't resisting the variation — he's worried about his boss's reaction. The PM reframes: "What if I prepare a detailed justification with independent geotechnical data? That would give you what you need to get approval." The rep agrees. The variation is approved.

### Software

A sales director is negotiating an enterprise contract. The buyer's procurement lead is aggressive: "Your price is 30% above market."

**Without EQ:** The sales director defends: "Our product is superior. Here's the feature comparison." The procurement lead becomes more aggressive. The negotiation becomes a price war.

**With EQ:** The sales director reads the procurement lead's stress (body language: neck touching, rapid speech, checking phone). The sales director uses a calibrated question: "What's driving the urgency on this?" The procurement lead reveals: "My CFO has mandated a 20% cost reduction across all vendors this quarter. I have to show progress." Now the sales director understands the constraint: it's not about the product's value — it's about the procurement lead's internal pressure. The sales director reframes: "I understand you need to show cost savings. What if we structure this as a 3-year contract with a 15% year-1 discount? That gives you the savings number you need, and we get a longer commitment." The procurement lead agrees.

### Healthcare

A hospital administrator is negotiating with a doctors' union over working conditions. The union rep is angry: "Your staffing ratios are unsafe and you know it."

**Without EQ:** The administrator responds defensively: "Our ratios meet regulatory standards." The union rep becomes more angry. The negotiation escalates toward a strike.

**With EQ:** The administrator reads the union rep's anger and fear (body language: red face, clenched fists, leaning forward aggressively). The administrator uses tactical empathy: "It sounds like you're genuinely worried about patient safety and your members' wellbeing." The union rep's posture softens: "We are. We've had three near-misses this month because nurses are stretched too thin." Now the administrator understands the interest: it's not about money — it's about safety. The administrator responds: "I take that seriously. Let's look at the near-miss data together and design a staffing plan that addresses safety within our budget constraints." The negotiation shifts from confrontation to collaboration.

## 5. Case Study: The Merger Negotiation That Emotion Almost Killed

### Situation

Two mid-sized pharmaceutical companies — PharmaA and PharmaB — were negotiating a merger. The deal made strategic sense: complementary product portfolios, combined R&D capability, and cost synergies of $200M/year. After 4 months of productive negotiation, the deal suddenly stalled.

### Problem

The CEOs of both companies had developed a personal animosity. In a joint press briefing, PharmaA's CEO made an offhand comment about PharmaB's "declining market share." PharmaB's CEO interpreted this as a public insult. The relationship deteriorated rapidly:
- PharmaB's CEO stopped taking PharmaA's CEO's calls
- PharmaB's team began rejecting every proposal from PharmaA
- PharmaA's CEO became frustrated and sent an aggressive email demanding a response
- PharmaB's CEO forwarded the email to his board with the comment: "This is how they treat partners"

The deal was 2 weeks from collapse.

### Root Cause Analysis

The deal stalled not because of substantive disagreement but because of emotional escalation:
1. **Trigger:** The "declining market share" comment (unintentional but insensitive)
2. **Interpretation:** PharmaB's CEO interpreted it as a deliberate insult (fundamental attribution error — assuming the worst about the other's intentions)
3. **Emotional escalation:** Anger → resentment → passive aggression → active hostility
4. **Behavioural escalation:** Stopped communicating → rejected proposals → sent aggressive email → shared email with board
5. **Identity dimension:** PharmaB's CEO felt his company (and by extension, himself) was being disrespected — triggering a deep identity threat

### Resolution Strategy

The lead advisors brought in an executive coach who specialised in emotional intelligence:

**1. De-escalation:**
- The coach met with each CEO separately to understand their emotional state
- PharmaA's CEO: frustrated, confused, felt his comment was misinterpreted
- PharmaB's CEO: angry, disrespected, felt the merger was a takeover not a partnership
- The coach validated both emotions without taking sides

**2. Perspective-taking:**
- The coach helped each CEO see the situation from the other's perspective
- PharmaA's CEO realised the comment was genuinely hurtful, even if unintentional
- PharmaB's CEO realised the comment was careless, not calculated

**3. The apology:**
- PharmaA's CEO called PharmaB's CEO: "I want to apologise for my comment about market share. It was insensitive and I understand why it upset you. That wasn't my intention, but intention doesn't equal impact. I value this partnership and I want to get back on track."

**4. Rebuilding trust:**
- Both CEOs agreed to a face-to-face dinner (no advisors, no agenda)
- They agreed on communication norms: no negative comments about each other's companies, concerns raised privately not publicly
- They agreed to a weekly call to maintain alignment

### Outcome

- The merger was completed 6 weeks later
- Both CEOs developed a working relationship that, while not warm, was professional and respectful
- The combined company achieved $180M in synergies (90% of target)
- The coach remained as a neutral facilitator for the first 6 months post-merger

### Lessons Learned

1. **Emotions can kill deals that make perfect strategic sense.** The substantive case for the merger was strong — but emotional escalation nearly destroyed it.
2. **Unintentional insults cause real damage.** PharmaA's CEO didn't intend to insult, but the impact was real. Intention doesn't equal impact.
3. **The fundamental attribution error fuels conflict.** PharmaB's CEO assumed the comment was deliberate (attributing to malice what was actually carelessness).
4. **A genuine apology can reset a relationship.** PharmaA's CEO's apology — with acknowledgement, responsibility, and commitment — was the turning point.
5. **EQ interventions should happen early.** The coach was brought in at 2 weeks from collapse. If the emotional dynamics had been addressed when the comment was first made, the deal would never have stalled.
6. **Communication norms prevent escalation.** Agreeing to raise concerns privately and never criticise publicly created a safe space for disagreement.

## 6. Building Trust Through EQ

### The Trust Equation

Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Intimacy) / Self-Orientation

- **Credibility:** Do you know what you're talking about? (Expertise, data, track record)
- **Reliability:** Do you do what you say? (Consistency, follow-through, predictability)
- **Intimacy:** Do you create emotional safety? (Empathy, discretion, personal connection)
- **Self-orientation:** Are you focused on yourself or on both parties? (Lower self-orientation = higher trust)

**EQ application:** EQ directly enhances intimacy (through empathy and social awareness) and reduces self-orientation (through genuine interest in the other party's perspective). Both increase trust.

### The Trust-Building Sequence

1. **Before the negotiation:** Research the other party. Understand their business, their challenges, their industry. Come prepared with relevant insights, not just your agenda.

2. **Opening:** Build rapport. Find genuine common ground — not manufactured small talk. "I saw your company's announcement about [topic]. How is that progressing?"

3. **During negotiation:** Listen more than you speak. Ask questions that show genuine interest. Use tactical empathy. Label emotions. Don't interrupt.

4. **After negotiation:** Follow through on every commitment. Send the promised information. Make the introduction you offered. Trust is built through consistency over time.

### Repairing Broken Trust

When trust is damaged (by a broken promise, a perceived betrayal, or a misunderstanding):

1. **Acknowledge the damage:** "I understand that my action damaged your trust."
2. **Take responsibility:** "I should have [done differently]."
3. **Explain without excusing:** "Here's what happened. I'm not using this as an excuse — I take responsibility."
4. **Commit to change:** "Going forward, I will [specific change]."
5. **Give it time:** Trust is rebuilt through consistent behaviour over time, not through a single conversation.

## 8. Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Treating Emotion as Weakness

**Why it occurs:** Professional culture often equates emotion with irrationality.

**How experts avoid it:** They recognise that emotion drives decisions — rationalisation follows. They manage emotions rather than suppressing them. They use emotional awareness as a strategic advantage.

### Mistake 2: Not Preparing for Emotional Triggers

**Why it occurs:** Professionals prepare for substantive issues but not emotional dynamics.

**How experts avoid it:** They identify their triggers before the negotiation and prepare responses. They know what will make them angry, anxious, or defensive — and they have a plan.

### Mistake 3: Matching the Other Party's Emotion

**Why it occurs:** Emotional contagion — we unconsciously mirror the emotions of those around us.

**How experts avoid it:** They maintain emotional independence. If the other party is aggressive, they stay calm. If the other party is anxious, they provide reassurance. Matching emotion escalates; counterbalancing defuses.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Body Language

**Why it occurs:** Professionals focus on words and ignore non-verbal signals.

**How experts avoid it:** They actively read body language, tone, and pacing. They treat incongruence (words say one thing, body says another) as a signal to probe deeper.

### Mistake 5: Making Decisions While Emotionally Activated

**Why it occurs:** Pressure to respond immediately, or the emotional high of a "win."

**How experts avoid it:** They never make decisions while angry, anxious, or euphoric. They take a break, let emotions settle, and then decide. "I'd like to think about this overnight" is a powerful negotiation tool.

### Mistake 6: Faking Empathy

**Why it occurs:** Negotiators learn that empathy is effective and try to perform it.

**How experts avoid it:** They develop genuine curiosity about the other party's perspective. Faked empathy is usually detected and destroys trust. Genuine empathy builds it.

### Mistake 7: Not Repairing Emotional Damage

**Why it occurs:** After an emotional incident, both parties pretend it didn't happen.

**How experts avoid it:** They address emotional damage directly: "I think our last conversation got heated. I want to make sure we're in a good place to continue." Acknowledging and repairing damage strengthens the relationship.

### Mistake 8: Over-Reading Body Language

**Why it occurs:** After learning about body language, negotiators over-interpret every gesture.

**How experts avoid it:** They treat body language as signals to explore, not conclusions to act on. Crossed arms might mean resistance — or it might mean the room is cold. They use body language as a prompt to ask questions, not to make accusations.

### Mistake 9: Neglecting Post-Negotiation Relationship

**Why it occurs:** The deal is done and the negotiator moves on.

**How experts avoid it:** They maintain the relationship after the deal. They follow through on commitments. They check in periodically. Trust built during negotiation must be reinforced through post-negotiation behaviour.

### Mistake 10: Not Developing EQ Deliberately

**Why it occurs:** EQ is seen as a fixed trait rather than a developable skill.

**How experts avoid it:** They invest in EQ development: self-assessment, feedback, coaching, practice. Research shows EQ can be developed at any age through deliberate practice.

## 9. Advanced Expert Tips

### The Emotion Labeling Hierarchy

Different levels of emotional labeling produce different results:
- **Surface labels:** "It seems like you're concerned." → Acknowledges emotion, opens dialogue.
- **Deep labels:** "It seems like you're worried that if this timeline slips, your board will lose confidence in the project." → Names the specific fear, demonstrating deep understanding.
- **Triple labels:** "It seems like you're under pressure from your board, worried about the timeline, and concerned that we're not taking the risk seriously." → Names three emotions simultaneously, creating powerful validation.

Use deeper labels as trust builds. Start with surface labels, move to deep labels as the relationship develops.

### The "Late-Night FM DJ" Voice

Chris Voss recommends using a slow, low, soft voice — like a late-night FM DJ. This voice:
- Signals calm and confidence
- Activates the other party's parasympathetic nervous system (calming effect)
- Reduces adversarial tension
- Encourages the other party to speak more freely

Practice this voice before negotiations. It's not about manipulation — it's about creating a calm, productive emotional environment.

### The Accusation Audit

Before the negotiation, list every negative thing the other party might think about you or your proposal. Then address these proactively:

"I know you might think we're being greedy with this proposal. You might also think we don't understand your budget constraints. And you might think we're not taking your timeline pressure seriously. Let me address each of these."

This technique (from Voss) defuses negative emotions before they arise by acknowledging them first.

### Emotional Anchoring

Just as you can anchor on price, you can anchor on emotion. Start the negotiation by establishing a positive emotional tone:
- Express genuine enthusiasm for the opportunity
- Acknowledge the other party's expertise and perspective
- Frame the negotiation as a collaboration, not a battle
- Use humour (appropriate, genuine) to build rapport

The emotional anchor sets the tone for the entire negotiation. A positive opening makes subsequent disagreements easier to navigate.

## Key Takeaways

1. **EQ predicts negotiation success more than IQ.** Emotional intelligence is the single best predictor of negotiation outcomes.
2. **Develop all four EQ pillars.** Self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management.
3. **Know your triggers.** Identify what makes you angry, anxious, or defensive — and prepare responses.
4. **Use the 6-second rule.** When triggered, count to 6 before responding. Let the prefrontal cortex re-engage.
5. **Read body language as signals, not conclusions.** Use incongruence between words and body as a prompt to ask questions.
6. **Use tactical empathy and labeling.** Name the other party's emotions to defuse them and build trust.
7. **Never make decisions while emotionally activated.** Take a break, let emotions settle, then decide.
8. **Don't match the other party's emotion.** Counterbalance: stay calm when they're aggressive, provide reassurance when they're anxious.
9. **Build trust through consistency.** Credibility, reliability, intimacy, and low self-orientation.
10. **Develop EQ deliberately.** It's a skill, not a fixed trait. Invest in self-assessment, feedback, and practice.

## FAQ

### 1. Can emotional intelligence be learned, or is it innate?

Research consistently shows that EQ can be developed at any age through deliberate practice. Unlike IQ, which is relatively fixed, EQ involves skills — self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management — that can be improved through training, feedback, and practice. Start with self-assessment, seek feedback from trusted colleagues, and practice specific techniques in low-stakes negotiations before applying them in high-stakes situations.

### 2. How do I stay calm when the other party is deliberately provoking me?

Prepare in advance: identify your triggers and pre-commit your responses. During the negotiation: use the 6-second rule (count to 6 before responding), use tactical breathing (4-4-4-4), and name your emotion silently. If the provocation continues, take a break: "I'd like to take 10 minutes before we continue." Remember that deliberate provocation is a tactic — responding emotionally is doing what they want.

### 3. What is tactical empathy and how is it different from sympathy?

Tactical empathy is understanding the other party's feelings and perspective without necessarily sharing them or agreeing with them. Sympathy is feeling what they feel. Empathy is understanding what they feel and why. In negotiation, empathy is strategic — you use your understanding of their emotions to guide the negotiation productively. You don't have to agree with their position to understand their feelings.

### 4. How do I read body language accurately?

Look for clusters of signals, not isolated gestures. Crossed arms alone might mean the room is cold; crossed arms plus leaning back plus avoiding eye contact probably means resistance. Look for incongruence — when words say one thing and body says another. Treat body language as a signal to ask more questions, not as a conclusion to act on. And always consider cultural differences in body language.

### 5. What is labeling and how do I use it?

Labeling (from Chris Voss) is naming the other party's emotion as an observation: "It seems like you're concerned about the timeline." Labels start with "It seems like..." or "It sounds like..." — they're observations, not accusations. Labeling defuses emotions by making the other party feel heard. Don't label positive emotions ("It seems like you're happy") — that can seem patronising. Label negative or neutral emotions to defuse them.

### 6. How do I handle a negotiation where the other party is angry?

Stay calm — don't match their emotion. Use labeling: "It seems like you're really frustrated." Let them vent — don't interrupt. When they've finished, reflect back: "What I'm hearing is that you feel [X] because [Y]. Is that accurate?" Then redirect: "I understand your frustration. Let's talk about how we can resolve this." If the anger is escalating, take a break.

### 7. What is the accusation audit and when should I use it?

The accusation audit (from Chris Voss) is proactively addressing every negative thing the other party might think about you before they raise it. "You might think we're being greedy. You might think we don't understand your constraints." This defuses negative emotions by acknowledging them first. Use it at the opening of a negotiation or before presenting a proposal that you expect resistance to.

### 8. How do I build trust quickly in a negotiation?

Build trust through: credibility (demonstrate expertise and preparation), reliability (do what you say, even in small things), intimacy (show genuine interest and empathy), and low self-orientation (focus on their needs as well as yours). In the opening, find genuine common ground. Listen more than you speak. Follow through on every commitment, no matter how small.

### 9. What if I detect that the other party is lying?

Don't accuse directly. Instead, present contradictory evidence and ask for explanation: "I notice that the market data shows a different picture. Can you help me understand the discrepancy?" If deception is confirmed and material, consider whether the relationship is worth continuing. Document the behaviour. In some cases, walking away is the right response to demonstrated dishonesty.

### 10. How do I manage my emotions in email negotiations?

Email is particularly challenging because it lacks tone and body language. Assume positive intent — don't read anger into neutral language. Wait 24 hours before responding to a provocative email. Draft your response, then re-read it before sending. If the email exchange is becoming emotional, switch to phone or video: "I think we'd be more productive discussing this on a call."

### 11. How does EQ apply to virtual negotiations?

In virtual negotiations, you have fewer emotional signals (no full body language, limited eye contact). Compensate by: using video (not just audio), paying extra attention to tone and facial expressions, asking more questions to check understanding, taking more frequent breaks, and using explicit verbal acknowledgment ("I hear what you're saying, and I understand why that's important to you").

### 12. Can high EQ be a disadvantage in negotiation?

High EQ without strong analytical skills can lead to over-accommodation — being so attuned to the other party's emotions that you concede too much. EQ must be paired with strong preparation, clear objectives, and a firm reservation price. The best negotiators have both high EQ and high analytical capability — they understand the emotions but make decisions based on data and strategy.
