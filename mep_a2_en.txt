## Introduction to Consulting Services in Construction

**Construction consulting services** encompass the professional engineering, advisory, and management expertise provided by specialised firms or individuals throughout the project lifecycle — from feasibility and concept design through construction supervision and handover. The consultant acts as the **technical representative of the employer/owner**, ensuring that the project is delivered to the required quality, cost, and programme standards.

Under international contracts such as **FIDIC (Federation Internationale des Ingenieurs-Conseils)**, the consultant is formally designated as **"The Engineer"** — a role with defined powers, duties, and responsibilities for contract administration, technical decisions, and dispute resolution.

---

## 1. Types of Consulting Services in Construction

### 1.1 Pre-Construction Consulting

**Feasibility Studies:**
- Site assessment: topographical survey, geotechnical investigation, environmental impact assessment
- Market analysis: demand projection, comparable projects, absorption rate
- Financial modelling: development cost estimate, IRR, NPV, sensitivity analysis
- Risk assessment: political, economic, environmental, technical risks
- Deliverable: feasibility report with go/no-go recommendation

**Concept and Schematic Design:**
- Architectural concept: massing studies, space programming, circulation analysis
- Structural concept: structural system selection (RC, steel, composite), span analysis, foundation type
- MEP concept: system zoning, central plant location, energy strategy
- Sustainability strategy: LEED/GSAS target, renewable energy integration, water conservation
- Deliverable: concept design report, schematic drawings, outline specification

**Detailed Design:**
- Architectural: detailed floor plans, elevations, sections, finishes schedule, door/window schedule
- Structural: structural calculations, reinforcement drawings, steel connection details, foundation design
- MEP: system design calculations, equipment selection, layout drawings, control schematics
- Tender documentation: bills of quantities, technical specifications, tender drawings
- Deliverable: issued for construction (IFC) drawings, specification books, BOQ

### 1.2 Construction-Phase Consulting

**Construction Supervision:**
- Resident engineer and inspector team on-site
- Review and approval of contractor submittals (materials, shop drawings, method statements)
- Inspection of works: setting out, rebar, formwork, concrete pour, MEP installation
- Quality control: witness testing, review of test results, approval of non-conformance reports (NCR)
- Programme monitoring: track progress against baseline, identify delays, recommend recovery
- Cost control: verify interim payment certificates, approve variations, manage claims
- Safety: review contractor HSE plan, audit site safety, investigate incidents
- Deliverable: monthly progress reports, payment certificates, snagging list, completion certificate

**Project Management Consulting:**
- Overall project management: scope, time, cost, quality, risk, procurement, communication
- Stakeholder management: coordination between owner, consultant, contractor, authorities
- Change management: evaluate change requests, impact analysis, recommend approvals
- Document control: maintain master document register, control revisions
- Reporting: executive dashboards, KPI tracking, earned value analysis
- Deliverable: project management plan, monthly reports, change order log, risk register

### 1.3 Post-Construction Consulting

**Commissioning and Handover:**
- Witness commissioning tests for MEP systems
- Review O&M manuals and as-built drawings
- Prepare snagging/defects list
- Issue practical completion certificate
- Manage defects liability period (typically 12 months)

**Post-Occupancy Evaluation:**
- Building performance evaluation: energy consumption, indoor air quality, thermal comfort
- Occupant satisfaction survey
- System optimisation: BMS tuning, HVAC balancing, lighting adjustments
- Energy audit: compare actual vs design energy performance
- Deliverable: post-occupancy evaluation report, optimisation recommendations

## 2. The Consultant's Role Under FIDIC Contracts

### 2.1 FIDIC Contract Types

**Red Book (Construction):**
- FIDIC Conditions of Contract for Construction
- Used where the employer provides the design; contractor executes the works
- The Engineer administers the contract, issues drawings, certifies payments

**Yellow Book (Plant and Design-Build):**
- FIDIC Conditions of Contract for Plant and Design-Build
- Used where the contractor designs and builds
- The Engineer reviews and approves contractor's design

**Silver Book (EPC/Turnkey):**
- FIDIC Conditions of Contract for EPC/Turnkey Projects
- Contractor takes total responsibility for design and construction
- The Engineer's role is reduced; employer's representative may replace the Engineer
- Fixed price, fixed date — contractor bears most risk

**Emerald Book (Underground Works):**
- FIDIC Conditions of Contract for Underground Works
- Used for tunnels, mining, and subsurface construction
- Risk-sharing framework for ground conditions

### 2.2 The Engineer's Duties and Powers Under FIDIC

**Contract Administration:**
- Issue notices of commencement and possession of site
- Review and approve contractor's programme (Clause 8)
- Issue drawings and instructions (Clause 3)
- Review and approve method statements and risk assessments
- Certify payments: interim payment certificates (Clause 14), final payment certificate

**Technical Decisions:**
- Determine time extensions (Clause 8.4) — evaluate delay claims
- Evaluate variations (Clause 13) — assess cost and time impact
- Resolve disputes: initial decision in case of disagreement (Clause 20)
- Determine rates for new work items or variations
- Approve materials and equipment submittals

**Quality and Compliance:**
- Inspect and test the works (Clause 7) — witness testing, approve test results
- Issue non-conformance reports (NCR) for defective work
- Order removal of defective work (Clause 7.5)
- Issue taking-over certificate (Clause 10) upon completion
- Issue performance certificate at end of defects liability period

**Financial:**
- Evaluate and certify contractor's payment claims
- Determine variations and their cost impact
- Manage claims from both employer and contractor
- Administer retention money and advance payment guarantees
- Final account settlement

### 2.3 Consultant Independence and Impartiality

Under FIDIC, the Engineer must act **impartially** between employer and contractor:
- Cannot favour the employer in technical decisions
- Must apply the contract fairly and consistently
- Decisions must be based on contract provisions, not on employer preferences
- If the Engineer is also the designer, potential conflict of interest must be managed
- In EPC contracts (Silver Book), the Engineer's impartiality role is reduced

## 3. Value Engineering in Construction Consulting

### 3.1 Value Engineering Methodology

Value Engineering (VE) is a systematic approach to improving project value by analysing functions and reducing costs without sacrificing quality, reliability, or performance.

**VE Job Plan (per SAVE International):**

1. **Information Phase:**
   - Gather project data: design drawings, specifications, cost estimates
   - Identify functions: what does each element do? (verb-noun format)
   - Determine costs: allocate costs to functions

2. **Function Analysis Phase:**
   - Classify functions: basic (must have), secondary (support), unnecessary
   - Function cost-worth analysis: compare cost to worth
   - Identify high-cost/low-worth functions as VE targets

3. **Creative Phase:**
   - Brainstorm alternatives: how else can each function be achieved?
   - No judgement during brainstorming — quantity over quality
   - Generate 20-50 alternatives per function

4. **Evaluation Phase:**
   - Screen alternatives: feasibility, cost impact, schedule impact, risk
   - Rank alternatives: matrix scoring (cost, performance, schedule, risk)
   - Select top 3-5 alternatives for development

5. **Development Phase:**
   - Develop selected alternatives: sketches, cost estimates, technical analysis
   - Life-cycle cost analysis: initial cost + operating cost + maintenance + disposal
   - Risk assessment: what could go wrong?

6. **Presentation Phase:**
   - Present recommendations to owner and design team
   - Provide implementation plan and cost savings
   - Obtain approval for implementation

### 3.2 Common Value Engineering Opportunities

**Architectural:**
- Reduce floor-to-floor height: saves structure, façade, MEP vertical runs
- Standardise room sizes: reduces formwork repetition, improves construction efficiency
- Simplify façade geometry: reduces curtain wall cost and installation time
- Optimise window-to-wall ratio: balances daylighting with cooling load

**Structural:**
- Convert from steel to composite construction: 15-20% cost saving on typical spans
- Optimise column grid: longer spans cost more; shorter spans reduce flexibility
- Use high-strength concrete: reduces column size and rebar quantity
- Value-engineer foundations: raft vs pile vs combined — geotechnical re-evaluation

**MEP:**
- Variable refrigerant flow (VRF) vs chilled water: VRF simpler, lower first cost for small buildings
- LED lighting with daylight harvesting: 30-50% lighting energy reduction
- Right-size central plant: avoid over-design; use diversity factors per ASHRAE
- Heat recovery: exhaust air heat recovery reduces cooling load 15-25%

**Construction Methods:**
- Pre-fabrication: bathroom pods, MEP racks, wall panels — 20-30% schedule reduction
- Design for manufacture and assembly (DfMA): standardise connections and components
- Modular construction: 40-50% schedule reduction for repetitive building types

## 4. Construction Supervision in Detail

### 4.1 Site Supervision Team Structure

**Project Director (Consultant):**
- Overall responsibility for supervision contract
- Client interface and reporting
- Resolution of major technical and contractual issues

**Resident Engineer (RE):**
- Day-to-day management of site supervision
- Review and approval of shop drawings and submittals
- Technical queries and site instructions
- Chair site meetings

**Discipline Engineers:**
- Structural Engineer: rebar inspection, concrete quality, formwork approval
- MEP Engineer: mechanical and electrical installation inspection
- Architectural Engineer: finishes, façade, fit-out quality control

**Inspectors:**
- Continuous site presence for monitoring works
- Daily inspection reports
- Witness testing and sampling

### 4.2 Inspection and Test Plan (ITP)

The ITP is a document that defines how and when inspections and tests will be performed:

**ITP Structure:**
- Activity: e.g., concrete pour, ductwork installation, cable pulling
- Reference specification: clause numbers and standards
- Inspection method: visual, dimensional, laboratory test, non-destructive test
- Acceptance criteria: tolerance, test result thresholds
- Witness level: Hold Point (must wait for inspector), Witness Point (inspector notified), Review Point (document review)

**Typical ITP Hold Points:**
- Excavation completion before blinding concrete
- Rebar inspection before concrete pour (pre-pour inspection)
- Waterproofing before backfilling
- Pressure test of pipework before concealment
- Megger test of cables before energisation
- Fire damper installation before ceiling closure

### 4.3 Quality Control Documentation

**Inspection Requests (IR):**
- Contractor submits IR for each hold point
- Inspector attends, performs inspection, records results
- IR approved = proceed; IR rejected = rectify and resubmit

**Non-Conformance Reports (NCR):**
- Issued when work does not meet specification
- Describes the non-conformance, location, and reference
- Contractor proposes corrective action
- Consultant approves corrective action
- Re-inspection after correction
- NCR closed when correction verified

**Site Instructions (SI):**
- Issued by consultant to contractor for clarifications, additional work, or corrections
- May result in variation order if cost/time impact
- Numbered sequentially for tracking

**Method Statements (MS):**
- Contractor's proposed method for executing specific activities
- Reviewed and approved by consultant before work starts
- Includes: scope, resources, sequence, safety measures, quality control
- Critical for: crane lifts, deep excavation, hot work, confined space entry

## 5. Consultant-Contractor Relationship and Communication

### 5.1 Communication Protocols

**Formal Communication Channels:**
- Site instructions (SI): consultant to contractor
- Technical queries (TQ): contractor to consultant
- Inspection requests (IR): contractor to consultant
- Non-conformance reports (NCR): consultant to contractor
- Variation orders (VO): consultant to contractor (with employer approval)
- Payment certificates: consultant to contractor and employer

**Site Meetings:**
- Weekly progress meeting: RE, contractor project manager, discipline leads
- Monthly progress meeting: project director, employer representative, contractor senior management
- Technical coordination meeting: discipline engineers from both sides
- Safety committee meeting: HSE representatives from all parties

**Reporting:**
- Daily inspection reports: from inspectors to RE
- Weekly progress report: from RE to project director and employer
- Monthly progress report: from consultant to employer — includes progress, cost, quality, safety, risks, look-ahead
- Exception report: for significant events (delays, accidents, claims)

### 5.2 Dispute Resolution Process

1. **Engineer's Decision:** Dispute referred to the Engineer for initial determination (FIDIC Clause 20)
2. **Amicable Settlement:** If either party disagrees with Engineer's decision, attempt settlement within 28 days
3. **Dispute Adjudication Board (DAB):** If settlement fails, refer to DAB for binding decision
4. **Arbitration:** If DAB decision is not accepted, proceed to arbitration per contract rules
5. **Litigation:** Last resort, if arbitration clause not invoked

## 6. Technical Advisory Services

### 6.1 Independent Technical Review

Consultants provide independent technical review for:
- Peer review of designs by other consultants
- Value engineering assessment of existing designs
- Constructability review: can the design be built efficiently?
- Sustainability assessment: LEED/GSAS compliance review
- Risk assessment: technical, programme, and cost risks

### 6.2 Expert Witness Services

- Dispute resolution support: provide technical opinion in arbitration or litigation
- Delay analysis: forensic schedule analysis using time impact analysis, windows analysis
- Cost claim analysis: quantum and causation assessment
- Defect investigation: root cause analysis of structural, MEP, or envelope failures
- Standard of care assessment: did the designer/contractor meet professional standards?

### 6.3 Digital Consulting

- BIM implementation strategy: standards, workflows, software selection
- Digital twin development: 3D model + real-time data integration
- Project management information systems (PMIS): selection and implementation
- Drone survey and photogrammetry: progress monitoring, stockpile measurement
- Reality capture: laser scanning for as-built verification

## 7. Consulting Fee Structures

### 7.1 Common Fee Types

**Lump Sum (Fixed Fee):**
- Fixed price for defined scope of services
- Suitable for well-defined scopes (detailed design, supervision)
- Risk: scope changes require change orders
- Advantage: cost certainty for client

**Time-Based (Hourly/Daily Rate):**
- Payment based on time spent by consultant's staff
- Suitable for undefined scopes (advisory, expert witness)
- Rates vary by role: Director $200-400/hr, Senior Engineer $150-250/hr, Inspector $80-150/hr
- Requires timesheet approval and detailed records

**Percentage of Construction Cost:**
- Fee calculated as percentage of total construction cost
- Typical ranges: 4-8% for design, 3-6% for supervision, 8-14% for combined design+supervision
- Scale: larger projects have lower percentage (economies of scale)
- Risk: no incentive for cost control; may encourage over-design

**Cost-Plus Fee:**
- Reimbursable expenses (travel, printing, sub-consultants) plus management fee
- Management fee: 10-20% of reimbursable expenses
- Used for open-ended assignments

### 7.2 Fee Benchmarks (Middle East, 2026)

| Service | Fee Range | Basis |
|---------|-----------|-------|

## 8. Regulatory and Ethical Considerations

### 8.1 Professional Registration and Licensing

- Consultants must be registered with local engineering councils (e.g., Saudi Council of Engineers, UAE Society of Engineers)
- Professional indemnity insurance: typically 10x annual fee or minimum $1M
- International accreditation: ISO 9001 (quality), ISO 14001 (environment), ISO 45001 (safety)

### 8.2 Code of Ethics

- **Integrity:** Act with honesty and fairness in all professional dealings
- **Competence:** Only undertake work within area of competence
- **Confidentiality:** Protect client's confidential information
- **Conflict of Interest:** Disclose any potential conflicts; avoid dual roles
- **Sustainability:** Promote sustainable design and construction practices
- **Public Safety:** Prioritise public health, safety, and welfare

## Conclusion

Construction consulting services are essential for delivering complex projects to the required standards of quality, cost, and time. The consultant's role spans the entire project lifecycle — from feasibility and design through supervision and handover. Under FIDIC contracts, the Engineer (consultant) plays a pivotal role in contract administration, technical decision-making, and dispute resolution.

For expert engineers, the consulting field demands not only deep technical knowledge but also strong communication, negotiation, and project management skills. As the construction industry embraces digital transformation, consultants must also master BIM, digital twins, and data-driven project management to remain competitive and deliver value to their clients.
